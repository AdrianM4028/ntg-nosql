For me, choosing a text for doing a play has been about
what I feel like sharing at any given point of time; or what
is troubling me at that time. After a long wait, I decided to deal
with the impact of political events on personal relationships,
particularly in the context of communal events. This was
partly because my personal experience of an हूँभाटा- communal
marriage gave me a glimpse into the kind of insecurities
religious minorities possibly develop in the climate of
increasing communalization stemming from the Babri Masjid
demolition, the Bombay blasts and the horrific Gujarat
pogrorn. As international events like 9/11 and the Iraq War
overpowered our consciousness, I felt the isolation and
stereotyping ofone community acquire global status.
It was at this juncture that I happened to read Geetanjali's
beautifully understated and nuanced novel Hamara Shahar... I
initially felt the topic may appear outdated, but when I look
around I find the alienation of religious minorities continuing
to grow, with their humiliation becoming increasingly and
insidiously institutionalized. In fact, I find their isolation has
become more consolidated, their identities more judged and
their integrity more suspected. In such a context my decision
to do this play is based on the need to question, re’examine
and revisit unspoken biases that govern the dynamics of
human identities and relationships.

The process of the play proved to be exciting as the novel
provided evocative images, collages and soundscapes. The
repertory team worked on improvisations that opened up the
text for multiple readings. In fact, the entire design of the
production has been evolved by this team. Geetanjali's novel
encompasses a large canvas through a montage like structure,
bringing in small details that make the narrative very rich.
While the play tries to follow the same kind of structure, it

does not enjoy the same leisure to include the gradual change
in the arnbience and imperceptible shifts in relationships.
