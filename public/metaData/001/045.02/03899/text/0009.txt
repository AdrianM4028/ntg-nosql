Directors Note
Anuradha Kapur

This production of Sweet Bird of Youth by Tennessee Williams has been the
result of a sort of acting workshop that has sought to address the problems of
text, sub-text, physical action, and physicalisation. The issues revolve around
the process of creating a role and of developing a psychological world that

manifests itself in the body with boldness and truth. In this process the students
have been ably tutored by Harish Khanna.

The play has been entirely improvised and has taken shape through exercises
that have attempted to open up the text for the students. The text itself has also
been edited, translated and developed through the month-long rehearsal by
Raj esh Tailang in his interaction with the actors while they improvised. He has
sought to develop an idiom that is both racy and colloquial like the “languages”
used by Tennessee Williams.

The design students have also been guided by Vishal Dar, Kriti V. Sharma, and
Ram Rahman on their various projects on design, costume and poster-making
respectively, with the guides seeking to help them realize their ideas. We see
these performances as a collaborative process where actors dialogue with the
space and the designers dialogue with them to resolve performance problems.

Tennessee Williams‘s play is about youth, of course, but it is also about ageing,
about acting, about deception and the chasing of a fading dream. While working
on the text, I hope we have succeeded in addressing at least some ofthese issues.
Anuradha Kapur
