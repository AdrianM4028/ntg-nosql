Mahesh Elkunchwar‘s Virasat is a generational saga in which
the lives of the Deshpandes of Dharangaon are mapped on
the changing landscape of India. What interested us as a
company was the epic scale of the trilogy — The Old Stone
Mansion, The Pool and Apocalypse. Epic narratives of modern
India are rare. And Mahesh Elkunchwar's epic is different from
the structure ofthe conventional epic in as much as it does not
have a march of heroic characters. Characters in Virasat are
defined by the quotidian. Everyday objects determine the
width and breadth of a lifetime.

The attempt to do these three plays as a running text with one
interval was to experience duration and history as mapped on
character. Equally it was to dialogue with Mahesh Elkunchwar‘s
experiment with form. In a remarkable journey Elkunchwar
takes us deftly through the conventions of realism, then
centralizes the metaphor of a pond (life, plenitude as also
decomposition and detritus) as a defining trope, and finally
writes up three classic soliloquies - philosophical dialogue
with aspects of self / soul, in the last play.
In developing a grammar of performance we attempted a sort
of hyper realist score of action. As much time as it takes to
cook basic food, to serve, to wash dishes, to put away clothes,
is more or less done, in real time, as it were.
This line ofthoug ht also guides the placement ofthe audience.
The spectators are set in various spaces close to the actors,
within the mansion. Are we members of the community?
Outsiders? Each view is slightly different and gives a different
sense of the house and its focuses of activity.
